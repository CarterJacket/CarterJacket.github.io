The green arithmetic
of electric vehicles

An EV is only as clean as the grid that charges it. So I pulled the EPA's 2022 grid emissions data and ran the numbers for all 50 states: in eight of them, a battery-electric car emits more operating carbon than a Toyota Prius hybrid. The same math shows the IRA's now-expired $7,500 clean-vehicle credit bought five times more carbon reduction in Vermont than in West Virginia, and whether any replacement gets designed better depends on understanding that gap.

The premise

"Electric" is not the same as "clean"

The popular story is simple: buy an EV, cut your carbon. But an electric car has no tailpipe, which means all of its operating emissions happen somewhere else, at the power plant that fills its battery. A car charged on Vermont's hydro and nuclear grid is nearly carbon-free to drive. The identical car charged in West Virginia, where most electricity still comes from coal, carries the emissions of that coal with every mile.

I wanted to test where that distinction actually flips the answer. To keep the comparison fair, I measured operating emissions on both sides: grid generation for the EV, tailpipe combustion for the gas cars. Neither figure includes vehicle or battery manufacturing. The result is a clean apples-to-apples read on what comes out of driving the car, mile after mile.

The Question

In which states does an electric vehicle actually beat a hybrid on carbon, and where does the cleanest choice depend entirely on the wires in the wall?

Finding 1

The dirty EV paradox

A Toyota Prius burns gasoline, but it sips it: at 52 MPG combined it emits about 0.38 lb of CO2 per mile, the same in every state. An EV's per-mile emissions instead scale with the local grid. Run the breakeven and the line lands at roughly 1,200 lb CO2 per MWh: above that, the EV is dirtier to drive than the hybrid. Eight states sit above the line, all of them coal-heavy grids.

The read: the EV is the cleaner car almost everywhere, but "almost" is the headline. In West Virginia, Wyoming, Kentucky, Indiana, Utah, Missouri, North Dakota and Hawaii, a buyer who wants the lowest-carbon drive today would do better in a hybrid. And because this counts operating emissions only, adding battery manufacturing would push the breakeven further against EVs, so eight states is the optimistic floor, not the ceiling.
Finding 2

The IRA's unequal carbon ROI

The same grid gap warped federal policy. The Inflation Reduction Act's $7,500 clean-vehicle credit ran from 2023 until it was repealed by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, expiring for new vehicle purchases after September 30, 2025. While it was in effect, the carbon it bought varied enormously by state. Divide $7,500 by the lifetime CO2 an EV actually avoided versus an average new gas car, and the cost of abatement swings from about $149 per ton in Vermont to $808 per ton in West Virginia, a 5x spread for the exact same check.

Dollars of federal credit per metric ton of lifetime CO2 avoided (vs. an average 26 MPG new car over 150,000 miles). Green is efficient, red is expensive. Hover for each state's figure.

The policy angle: the EPA's social cost of carbon is about $190 per ton, the line above which a dollar of abatement returns more than it costs. By that test the credit was a clear win in clean-grid states, roughly break-even in the middle of the country, and a poor carbon buy in the dirtiest-grid states, where it cost four times the social-cost benchmark to avoid a ton. The credit has since expired, but the math is a blueprint: any future replacement that scales the incentive with local grid cleanliness would buy the same carbon reduction for materially less federal spending.
Finding 3

Adoption and clean grids do not line up

If the market were sorting itself for climate impact, EVs would cluster where the grid is cleanest. The pull exists but it is weak: registrations per 1,000 residents correlate with grid carbon intensity at just r = -0.33, so grid cleanliness explains only about a tenth of the variation in adoption. The rest is income, policy and charging infrastructure. Several dirty-grid states have respectable adoption, and plenty of clean-grid states lag.

Each point is a state. Red points are the eight states where an EV is dirtier than a Prius. Adoption (vertical axis) is only weakly related to grid cleanliness (horizontal axis), at r = -0.33; the spread is explained mostly by wealth, incentives and infrastructure.

What it adds up to

Three things the numbers actually say

8

The advice is local

"Buy an EV to cut carbon" is right in 42 states and wrong in 8. The cleanest choice depends on the grid behind the outlet, not the badge on the car.

5x

Flat credits, uneven returns

The same $7,500 bought five times more carbon reduction in Vermont than in West Virginia. The credit has since expired; any replacement that weights by grid cleanliness would stretch the budget much further.

-0.33

Adoption barely tracks the grid

Where EVs sell is only weakly tied to where they are cleanest. Decarbonizing the grid, not just selling cars, is what makes every EV better.

Method and assumptions

Operating-emissions comparison built in Python (pandas, numpy) with interactive maps in Plotly. EV per-mile emissions use each state's 2022 grid output rate from EPA eGRID, at 0.315 kWh per mile at generation (0.30 kWh/mi real-world consumption grossed up ~5% for transmission and distribution losses). Gas baselines use the EPA tailpipe factor of 19.59 lb CO2 per gallon: a Toyota Prius at 52 MPG combined and an average new car at 26 MPG (EPA MY2022 real-world average). IRA efficiency divides the $7,500 credit (in effect 2023 through September 30, 2025, when it was repealed by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act) by lifetime CO2 avoided over 150,000 miles versus the average new car.

Boundary note, stated plainly: this is operating carbon only. It excludes vehicle and battery manufacturing (which favors the gas cars) and the upstream emissions of refining and transporting gasoline (which favors the EV). Including both would shift individual numbers, but the state-by-state ranking, the eight-state result, and the 5x policy spread are driven by grid intensity and are robust to the boundary choice. Adoption uses DOE AFDC 2023 registration counts normalized by Census 2023 population.
