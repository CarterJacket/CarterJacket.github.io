Rebuilding brick by brick

Facing softening revenues and a screen-first generation, the LEGO board had four ways to spend its capital and could not afford all of them. The temptation was the $365M digital prize. The disciplined answer was to fund the core first. Here is the capital allocation case we made to add $1 billion in profit over five years.

Role: Financial modeling and strategy, team of three. Team: Strategem, with Gavin Fung and Samantha Ng. Format: CPABC 2026 final round, March 2026.

Situation

A recovered brand facing a new kind of pressure

LEGO had already engineered one turnaround. After a 2004 crisis caused by over-expansion into theme parks, apparel, and video games, it rebuilt on operational excellence, licensed franchises like Star Wars and Marvel, and brand extensions like the 2014 LEGO Movie. By 2024 it ran 1,031 stores and a growing direct-to-consumer channel. The new threat is quieter: digital substitution is eroding children's attention, and the board needs a long-term plan, not another reactive expansion.

What is working

  • Operational excellence in forecasting and inventory
  • Product innovation through licensed franchises
  • Brand extensions into film and media
  • 1,031 stores plus a maturing D2C channel

What is pressing

  • Rising screen time competing for children's attention
  • Licensing is profitable but costly, royalties eat margin
  • Parents increasingly favour educational, problem-solving toys
  • The board must choose among four capital options, not fund all
The Key Question

What long-term financial and strategic moves should the LEGO board pursue to reverse declining revenues without overextending its capital?

Analysis

Four options, ranked by what the balance sheet can actually carry

Every option was tested on NPV, financing feasibility, and fit with LEGO's core. The digital prize is real but unaffordable now; geographic expansion destroys value; licensing pays but should not lead. The core is where the durable return sits.

OptionEconomicsVerdict
Brick-based playLead SKU-reduction savings plus added sales; $225M loan keeps coverage at 3.0 Fund first, the durable core return
Digital integrationPhase $365.2M NPV at an 8% WACC, but a $113.5M capital gap Defer past year 5, fund from brick cash
Licensing & media Positive NPV: $26.7M at $32 pricing, $11.0M at $35 Keep, but de-emphasize
Geographic expansion (China) Higher costs against lower revenue; incremental EBIT cuts coverage Reject, destroys value
Analyst read: The digital option has the biggest NPV and the biggest trap. Chasing it now blows the $300M annual cash limit and the 3.0 coverage covenant. Funding the core first generates the cash that makes digital affordable later.
Recommendation

Fund the core now, earn the right to go digital

A sequenced plan: rebuild around brick-based play to generate cash and protect the brand, then phase into a focused digital product once the balance sheet can support it.

1
Years 1 to 5

Return to the core

Allocate capital to brick-based play: new sets built on existing blocks, leaning on proven brands like LEGO Friends and Duplo, plus community design competitions and a new story aimed at teens. SKU reduction cuts production and distribution cost, the loan stays under $300M, and one-time costs sit below threshold.

  • Loan trimmed to $225M to reduce interest expense
  • Interest coverage ratio held at the required 3.0
  • Tracked on sales, design output, and competition traction
2
After year 5

Phase into digital

Once the core funds it, launch a focused problem-solving app for youth ages 9 to 12, a demographic parents already buy educational apps for. A slow, single-product phase-in (rather than a multi-project bet) keeps annual cash-out near the $300M limit and avoids the capital gap that made an immediate launch infeasible.

  • Funded from brick-based cash, not new high-risk debt
  • Phase-in targeted for completion by 2028
  • Opens a credible path into the gaming and education market
