AI didn't cause the layoffs.
But it's rewriting which jobs come back.

Between 2022 and 2025, the tech industry shed over half a million jobs. The headlines blamed AI — but the data tells a more interesting story about over-hiring, interest rates, and a structural shift in what companies hire for. Here's what the numbers actually say.

The Wave

Tech layoffs spiked when rates rose — not when ChatGPT launched

The largest cuts came in late 2022 and 2023, tracking the Fed's rate-hiking cycle and a pullback from pandemic-era over-hiring. ChatGPT launched in November 2022, but the AI narrative attached itself to a correction that was already underway.

Analyst read: The peak predates broad AI adoption. The story isn't "robots took the jobs" — it's a capital-cost correction. AI's real fingerprint shows up later, in the composition of who gets rehired.
The Shift

AI is becoming the stated reason — and exposure is uneven

Two things are happening at once. Companies increasingly name AI and "efficiency" in their layoff rationale, and the jobs most exposed to large language models are concentrated in specific functions — not evenly spread across the workforce.

So What?

Three takeaways for operators

If I were briefing a leadership team on this, here's where I'd land.

01

Don't confuse correlation with cause

The 2022–23 cuts were a rate-driven correction. Blaming AI obscures the real lesson: disciplined hiring beats boom-time over-hiring followed by painful resets.

02

Plan for compression, not elimination

AI rarely deletes a whole role — it raises the output per person. The teams that win redesign workflows around that leverage instead of just trimming headcount.

03

Redeployment is the strategy

The sectors growing through AI are reallocating talent to higher-value work. The competitive edge is reskilling speed, not cut depth.
