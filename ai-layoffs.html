AI didn't cause the layoffs.
But it's rewriting which jobs come back.

Between 2022 and 2025, the tech industry shed more than 700,000 jobs. The headlines blamed AI, but the data tells a more interesting story about over-hiring, interest rates, and a structural shift in what companies hire for. Here is what the public numbers actually say.

The Wave

Tech layoffs peaked in 2023, then fell as AI took off

The largest cuts landed in 2022 and 2023, tracking the Fed's rate-hiking cycle and a retreat from pandemic-era over-hiring. ChatGPT launched in November 2022, near the top of the wave, not the start of it. As AI adoption accelerated through 2024 and 2025, tech layoffs actually declined.

Analyst read: The peak predates broad AI deployment. This reads as a capital-cost correction, not an automation event. If AI were driving the cuts, you would expect the curve to climb as adoption rose. It did the opposite.
The Shift

Layoffs hit a post-2008 high in 2025, yet AI was named in only about 5%

Across all industries, US employers announced 1.2 million job cuts in 2025, the most since 2020. Of those, 54,836 cited AI as the reason. AI is real and rising as a stated driver, but it is still a small slice of a much larger restructuring story. Where AI does bite is uneven: exposure concentrates in specific functions, not evenly across the workforce.

Analyst read: Exposure is not the same as elimination. The most-exposed functions, like clerical, analytical, and writing work, are being compressed rather than erased. One person now does the work of several. That is a productivity story with a headcount side effect, concentrated far from the trades.
The Map

The sectors adopting AI fastest are the ones projected to grow

Plotting each sector's AI adoption rate against its projected employment growth reveals the real divide. High-adoption sectors like information, finance, and professional services are also projected to keep hiring. The only sectors projected to shrink, retail and manufacturing, are among the lowest AI adopters. Bubble size reflects total employment in the sector.

AI adoption vs. projected employment growth, by sector
X: AI adoption rate (Census BTOS). Y: projected annual employment change, 2023 to 2033 (BLS). Bubble size: sector employment (BLS).
Analyst read: If AI were a pure job destroyer, adoption and growth would move in opposite directions. They do not. The sectors leaning into AI are reshaping teams and still growing, while the shrinking sectors are losing jobs to e-commerce and offshoring, not to automation. The strategic question is not how many people to cut, it is what new value the same people can create.
So What?

Three takeaways for operators

If I were briefing a leadership team on this, here is where I would land.

01

Do not confuse correlation with cause

The 2022 and 2023 cuts were a rate-driven correction, and tech layoffs fell as AI adoption rose. Blaming AI obscures the real lesson: disciplined hiring beats boom-time over-hiring followed by painful resets.

02

Plan for compression, not elimination

AI rarely deletes a whole role. It raises the output per person in exposed functions. The teams that win redesign workflows around that leverage instead of just trimming headcount.

03

Adoption tracks growth, so redeploy

The sectors adopting AI fastest are still projected to hire. The competitive edge is reskilling speed and moving talent to higher-value work, not cut depth.

Methodology & sources

This is an independent analysis built to demonstrate data storytelling and structured thinking, not a forecast. Every charted figure comes from the public sources below. Tech layoff counts are from Layoffs.fyi. Total US job cuts and AI-attributed cuts are from the Challenger, Gray & Christmas monthly reports. Occupational AI exposure uses the task-level measures from Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock (2023). Sector AI adoption rates are from the US Census Bureau Business Trends and Outlook Survey, and projected employment growth and sector size are from the BLS Employment Projections, 2023 to 2033. Where a single clean series was not published at the sector level, values reflect the most recent figures reported by those sources.
