Dialing down on diesel

Fourteen remote network sites run on diesel generators, serving First Nations and Métis communities with no real alternative for connectivity. The economics say walk away; the mission says you cannot. With a flexible $6.5M budget over three years, the task was to cut diesel and emissions without ever cutting reliability for the people who depend on it most.

Situation

Low business return, high human necessity

These sites fail almost every ordinary investment test. They serve few users, generate little revenue, and need significant capital to keep running. But in remote and rural areas, connectivity is not optional. It underpins emergency communication, healthcare access, education, and economic participation. The real frame is equity versus efficiency, and for these communities equity has to win.

The efficiency view

  • Very few users per site and limited revenue
  • A small share of customers still needs heavy investment
  • Low return on capital by any standard business test

The equity view

  • Serves First Nations and Métis communities with no alternative
  • Enables emergency response, healthcare, and remote learning
  • A lost signal can mean losing access to essential needs
Complication

One budget, three very different kinds of site

The 14 sites are not interchangeable. They differ in grid proximity, weather, road access, and how catastrophic an outage would be. A single solution would either overspend on easy sites or underserve hard ones. We grouped them into three archetypes so each could get the right intervention.

Archetype 1: Near-grid

  • Year-round road access and maintained infrastructure
  • Faster repair response, lower operational complexity
  • Close enough to consider a grid connection

Archetype 2: Moderate

  • Seasonal delays from weather and road conditions
  • Heavily dependent on diesel logistics
  • Serves rural populations with limited options

Archetype 3: Severe / remote

  • Harsh weather and a fragile supply chain
  • Limited backup, high operational risk
  • A delayed signal can be life-threatening
The Key Question

What is the optimal three-year strategy to cut diesel use and emissions across all 14 sites while maintaining reliability, given a flexible $6.5M capital budget?

Analysis

Matching the technology to the terrain

Each archetype was scored on capital cost, operating cost, and diesel reduction across five options: solar, grid connection, operational efficiency, tower sharing, and wind. The right answer changes with sun, wind, and access. Near-grid sites can justify a full grid connection; remote sites lean on efficiency now and wind later.

Recommendation

A three-year phased rollout

Lead with the highest-impact, lowest-friction moves, then work toward the hardest sites. The early years prioritize environmental impact and quick wins; the final year completes decarbonization at the remote sites that need the most engineering.

Year 1

High-impact quick wins

Connect near-grid sites to the grid for permanent zero-diesel operation, shift moderate sites to solar and battery, and apply operational efficiency at remote sites as an immediate low-cost win that also preps them for a later wind retrofit.

Year 2

Finish and stabilize

Use year-one feedback to complete the grid and solar rollouts across remaining sites. No major new capital; the focus is operational stability, monitoring, and ongoing efficiency management at the remote archetype.

Year 3

Complete the portfolio

Retrofit the remote sites with wind, the best renewable for harsh, low-sun conditions, phased over the year. This $1.5M investment adds a further 40% diesel displacement and closes out the decarbonization plan.
